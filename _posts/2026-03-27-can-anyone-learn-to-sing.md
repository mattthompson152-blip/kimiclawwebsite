I've heard it hundreds of times: "I can't sing." Usually said with a laugh, like it's a settled fact.

Here's what I've learned after 25+ years of teaching: most people who think they can't sing actually can. They've just been told the wrong thing at the wrong time.

## Where the "Can't Sing" Belief Comes From

Often it's a childhood memory. A teacher who said "just mouth the words" in the school play. A parent who winced at a high note. A classmate who laughed. One moment, and singing became something other people did.

The problem isn't your voice — it's the belief you formed about it.

## What "Tone Deaf" Actually Means

Real tone deafness (amusia) affects less than 4% of people. If you can tell the difference between a fire truck siren and a bird chirping, you can distinguish pitch. That's the foundation of singing.

Most "tone deaf" people I've worked with just needed to learn how to control their voice. Nothing wrong with their ears.

## Singing Is a Skill, Not a Gift

Some people pick it up faster, sure. But that's true of everything — driving, cooking, tennis. The people who sing well mostly got there through practice, not magic.

I've taught hundreds of beginners. The ones who improve aren't the ones with "natural talent." They're the ones who show up and do the work.

## Starting From Scratch

If you've never sung, here's what actually happens in the first few lessons:

- You learn to make sounds on purpose instead of hoping they come out right
- You discover your range is probably bigger than you thought
- You realise those "bad" notes are just coordination issues, not character flaws
- You start hearing your own voice differently

It's not dramatic. It's gradual. But it happens.

## The Fear is Real (And Normal)

Singing makes you vulnerable. Your voice is personal. Being heard — really heard — can feel exposing.

I get it. That's why my beginner lessons are low-pressure. No performances, no judgments. Just you, learning how your voice works.
