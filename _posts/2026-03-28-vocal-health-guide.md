Unlike a guitar, you can't replace your voice. Damage it badly and the consequences can be serious. That's why vocal health matters — especially if you sing regularly.

After 25+ years working with professional performers, here's what actually works.

## Hydration (The Boring Truth)

Drink water. More than you think. Your vocal cords need to be hydrated from the inside — and that takes time. The water you drink now won't reach your cords for hours.

Aim for clear or pale yellow urine. That's your indicator.

Myth: Sipping water right before singing helps. It doesn't — not immediately, anyway.

## Warm-Ups Aren't Optional

Your vocal cords are muscles (technically folds, but they behave like muscles). You wouldn't run a marathon cold. Singing without warming up is the same.

Here's my 5-minute emergency warm-up:

1. **Breath awareness** (30 sec) — Deep belly breaths
2. **Lip trills** (1 min) — Sirens up and down
3. **NG slides** (1 min) — Smooth vocal cord connection
4. **Humming scales** (1 min) — Gentle activation
5. **Lip trills on a song** (1.5 min) — Apply to what you're singing

That's it. Five minutes prevents weeks of recovery from strain.

## Foods and Drinks: What Actually Matters

**Milk creates mucus?** Not exactly. Some people are sensitive to dairy; most aren't. Know your own body.

**Honey coats your throat?** It might feel soothing, but it doesn't reach your vocal cords. Placebo effect.

**What actually helps:**
- Room temperature water (cold can tighten things)
- Light meals before singing (not heavy)
- Avoid: alcohol before singing, excessive caffeine

## Silent Warm-Ups Exist

You can warm up without making sound:
- Deep breathing exercises
- Jaw and tongue stretches
- Yawning (relaxes the throat)
- Posture alignment

Perfect for warming up when you can't make noise — hotels, shared spaces, early mornings.

## When You're Sick

Hard truth: if your throat hurts, rest your voice. Singing on an injured voice can cause permanent damage.

Head cold but throat feels fine? Light singing might be okay. But be extra gentle.

Recovery protocol:
1. Complete vocal rest
2. Extra hydration
3. Steam (hot showers, humidifier)
4. Sleep
5. Patience

## When to See a Doctor

- Hoarseness lasting more than 2 weeks
- Pain when singing or speaking
- Complete voice loss
- Lumps you can feel

Find a laryngologist (voice specialist), not just a GP.
